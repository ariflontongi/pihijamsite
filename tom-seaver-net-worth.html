    Tom Seaver Net Worth: A Look at the Life and Legacy of “The Franchise”

    Tom Seaver, fondly referred to as “The Franchise,” was an American professional baseball player who is widely considered as one of the greatest pitchers in the history of the sport. Throughout his illustrious career, Seaver won numerous accolades, including three Cy Young Awards, a World Series championship, and induction into the Baseball Hall of Fame.

    Early Life and Education

    Tom Seaver was born on November 17, 1944, in Fresno, California. He grew up in a family of athletes; both his father and brother played college basketball, and his mother was a gymnast.

    Seaver attended Fresno High School, where he played both baseball and basketball. He was heavily recruited by college baseball programs, ultimately choosing to attend the University of Southern California (USC).

    While at USC, Seaver established himself as one of the best pitchers in college baseball. He helped lead the Trojans to two national championships and was the recipient of numerous individual awards, including the prestigious Golden Spikes Award.

    Career

    After his college career, Seaver was drafted by the Atlanta Braves in 1966. However, he was selected by the New York Mets in the 1966 Major League Baseball Draft as a first round selection, under a compensatory rule when the Braves signed Canadian umpire Bob Davidson away from the Mets.

    Seaver quickly established himself as one of baseball’s premier pitchers. In just his second season with the Mets, he won the National League Rookie of the Year Award, posting a 16-13 record with a 2.76 ERA.

    Throughout the rest of his career, Seaver continued to dominate on the mound. He won three Cy Young Awards (1969, 1973, and 1975), led the National League in strikeouts five times, and was named to the All-Star team 12 times. In 1969, he helped lead the Mets to their first-ever World Series championship.

    Seaver’s career also included stints with the Cincinnati Reds, Chicago White Sox, and Boston Red Sox. He retired in 1986 with a career record of 311-205 and a career ERA of 2.86.

    Seaver’s third marriage was to Nancy Lynn McIntyre, his high school sweetheart whom he reconnected with later in life. They remained married until Seaver’s death in 2020.

    On August 31, 2020, it was announced that Seaver had died in his sleep from complications of Lewy body dementia and COVID-19.
